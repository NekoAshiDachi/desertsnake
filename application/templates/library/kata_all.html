About kata



A kata is a prescribed sequence of karate techniques. It allows the practitioner to exercise the mind and body in preparation for combat. Certain combinations of techniques in the kata may be used in constructing combat applications for those techniques, and the range of movement is useful for increasing coordination, balance, strength, flexibility, and stamina.

型 : The kanji (Japanese character) for kata is composed of three radicals (characters). The upper left radical means shape, the upper right radical means cut, and the bottom radical means ground. A kata is a shape that cuts the ground. 形 is also frequently used for the word kata, and also means shape. [{{ ns.pubs['redmond']['n'] }}]

Origins

Kata may have originated long ago with the Chinese as an alternative method of recordkeeping to reading and writing. Quan, which were central training devices within their martial arts, were passed onto the Okinawans in the 18th and 19th centuries. As the Chinese revised their practices over time and did not limit themselves to single systems or instructors, specific kata lineage to systems remains largely unknown. Adding to the ambiguity, categorizing the subsequent Okinawan Naha and Shuri kata by their slow and fast speeds, respectively, may be anachronistic since each of the kata came from individual quan. [{{ ns.pubs['redmond']['n'] }}]

Examples of Chinese influences in modern kata:
  • Fujian White Crane, which descended from Shaolin boxing, influenced Sanchin, which was known as San Chian in White Crane. TODO - Wikipedia
  • These kata came from Fuzhou, China: Suparinpei (Peichurin), Sanseiryu, Sanchin, Seisan, Kururunfa, Seipai, Nipaipo, Niseishi, Useishi, Unshu, Sochin, Naihanchi, Channan (Pinans or Heians), Jutte, and possibly Kushanku [{{ ns.pubs['redmond']['n'] }}]

The names of kata may be older Okinawan names, or more recent Japanese names. Their kanji may not be the original kanji and may differ between instructors, so their meaning may have multiple interpretations. Gichin Funakoshi, the first Okinawan to teach karate in Japan, transliterated the original Okinawan names into the mainland Tokyo dialect in response to Japanese nationalism, but not all names were adapted. [{{ ns.pubs['redmond']['n'] }}]

Shotokan kata

There are about 150 distinct Okinawan and Japanese kata (including revisions and adaptations), most of which come from Okinawa. Kata created since the 1920s have been rejected by most instructors and associations as illegitimate, though with the exception of Tetsuhiko Asai's kata, students in the past were encouraged to create kata. [{{ ns.pubs['redmond']['n'] }}]

Shotokan is exclusively Shorin except for some of Shito-ryu’s Arakaki kata (Unsu, Sochin, and Nijushiho), which are reputed to have been imported into Shotokan by Funakoshi's son Gigo. The 100 Okinawan kata became the 21-32 of Shotokan (depending on the school) because 1) Funakoshi did not know all 100 kata, and/or 2) students chose not to do certain kata or forgot them. The Shotokai organization is stricter and endorses only the 15 of Funakoshi’s Karate-do Kyohan. [{{ ns.pubs['redmond']['n'] }}]

Applications

Kata used to contain grappling applications that were watered down or discarded with the rise of sport karate. Funakoshi, mainly a children's instructor, was not very skilled or knowledgeable about more aggressive karate applications. He also might have intentionally watered down techniques to popularize it as a family friendly sport. [{{ ns.pubs['redmond']['n'] }}]

Changes

Kata is supposed to be immutable, except for small changes by senior instructors supported by clear explanation. Funakoshi did introduce significant changes and was criticized sharply in Okinawa for that, and that was one reasons why he never returned to Okinawa. [{{ ns.pubs['yokota']['n'] }}]

Some of Funakoshi's changes:
  • Okinawan kata names became Japanese names.
  • Nekoashi-dachi replaced with koukutsu-dachi: Shorin-ryu does not have a koukutsu-dachi stance. Shito-ryu and Goju-ryu do have koukutsu-dachi, but it is more like a zenkutsu-dachi facing the opposite direction. A possible explanation is that Funakoshi depended on judo founder Jigoro Kano's support for the spread of karate in Japan, and the groin kick from nekoashi-dachi would too easily neutralize judo-ka before they could close distance. [{{ ns.pubs['yokota']['n'] }}]

  • Mae-geri replaced with yoko-geri-keage: Okinawan karate kicks are largely limited to mae-geri and mikazuki-geri (ushiro-geri in Unsu and a gedan kekomi in Bassai Dai being exceptions). Wado-ryu, whose founder left Shotokan around 1929, uses mae-geri in its kata, meaning that yoko-geri-keage was introduced after Funakoshi arrived in Japan. A couple possibilities why are that Funakoshi's university students may have requested more kicking variety in kata, and that yoko-geri-keage to the side is easier to do from koukutsu-dachi than from nekoashi-dachi in certain kata (e.g., Heian Nidan). Teaching and training



{% set RA = {"o": "WTKO" % o(8), "p": "Richard Amos" % p(32)} %}

Teaching

  • In a crowded class, "float" techniques and use the limited space as an opportunity to change timing and angle RA
  • Tell first time students to move by body/core first, and hands will follow, more or less. This is similar to how one changes distance and angle in sparring by not looking for the opponent’s hands RA

Training

  • In small spaces, pivot and turn actively, as if in kumite, drawing back/front foot in first as necessary in order to pivot sharply RA
  • Don't move head early; be ready as if attack may come from any direction RA
  • When not done with visible intention, stomping is permissible for competition-style kata; e.g. after hiki-ashi and during fast advances (one stepping technique quickly followed by another step) SKIF
