Kihon

Kihon means basic, but basic is anything but easy. The simplest movements can illustrate the areas requiring the most improvement. It is for this reason that the tradition of seemingly endless repetition of techniques exists. As practitioners at one dojo joked, "Mo ichi do means mo bettah" ("one more time means better"). Though at the same time, "perfect practice makes perfect technique." Kihon, then, is where all karate begins and ends.

This page is generally sectioned by what dominant parts of the body are being used, so by stances, arm techniques, leg techniques, and full body movements, similarly to how a new student might be introduced to karate. As this is part of my personal collection notes, I have the more fundamental points embolded, and my personal notes/goals (I'd like to believe that these can be anyone's) italicized. There may also be conflict between ideas belonging to different instructors and/or organizations, and such ideas have been superscripted to denote origin/credit.

Terminology

Concepts are described interchangeably, but otherwise embody the same concept of keeping movement low and direct:
  • Anatomically
    • Sagittal/lateral—dividing left and right
    • Frontal/coronal—dividing front and back
    • Transverse—dividing top and bottom
  • By Amos Sensei
    • Front body—often squeezing the pectorals and lats forward
    • Opposite body—squeezing the lats and ribs on the opposite side of one's technique forward
    • One point—I believe that one point is more specific than center of gravity since it refers to the position of shoulders and hips in addition to one's tantien

General technique

Basics

  • Seven important parts of techniques SL:
    1. Breathing
    2. Centerline
    3. Active stability
    4. Maximal contraction/expansion
    5. Snap/thrust
    6. Golgi effect
    7. Hip rotation: front/back
    • TODO: expand further
  • Even when doing techniques slowly, do not start off slow or delay technique and then speed up—maintain same speed; eventually improve initial acceleration.
  • Stay active and aware throughout technique, not just at start and end. RA

Coordination

  • Stiffness comes from misalignment and mistiming.
  • The centerline is on medial and frontal planes. By isolating the torso into different working segments, conserve energy and momentum while maintaining the technique’s direction upon the centerline.
  • The body operates as separate units, but all units must end at the same time. ID
  • Use arms for legs and legs for arms; this doesn't mean unncessary movement, but being mindful of the placement and usage of these parts in tandem with the respective arm/leg techniques. MM

Combinations

  • In combinations, sequence techniques to bounce from one technique to the next without pause/tension, and without reducing magnitude. RA
  • Emphasize pauses between combinations. RA
  • Consider the sequences in Heian Godan and Bassai Dai. RA

Stances

Placement

  • Head up, shoulders down; hara and tailbone pull towards each other. ID
  • Legs are not about shape, but as conduits for the body finding the ground. RA
  • Knee remains at same angle regardless of stance; only ankle angle changes. MM
  • Squeeze with front and inner thigh when feet are not crossed, otherwise with back and inner thigh. RA

Transitions

  • High center of gravity = speed; low center of gravity = power. MM Cats stay low in advancing on prey while prey moves upward (like antelopes) in attempting to escape. RA
  • Maintain direction of center of gravity's momentum in turns by not bringing the foot back. MM Avoid pushing off in opposite direction first or turning out knee/foot prior to movement; use inner thighs to begin movement; as if back foot about to get kicked from behind and focus on heel moving; drop from higher stance and stay low to move RA
  • Don't go sideways when stepping forward RA
  • Emulate Michael Jordan's coiled knees - coil down for transition between stances, moving into coil rather than moving pullback hand back; or coil downwards and towards, contracting ribs (see Heian Godan). RA

Basic stances

The main stances are zenkutsu-dachi (front stance), kōkutsu-dachi (back stance), and kiba-dachi (horse stance).

Upper body

General

  • Keep shoulders down by using latissimus dorsi muscles.
  • Better to move hands early, allowing more optionality. Opposite hand can float before block/punch, with hip; not as upward motion. RA

Hikite (pullback hand)

  • Use back to retract arm instead of elbow/fist; squeeze upper arm and lats together (as if squeezing armpit). RA
  • Hikite protects the soft area between the lowest rib and the hip bone. MM Hikite is ½-way between armpit & hip, with elbow pulled towards back, back tight, and knuckles to forearm straight. Causes same torso side to contract; other side expands; whips. ID

Punches

  • Can turn hip in opposite direction first to get hip twist with punch. MM,  Naka DE 2014
  • Use lats to fully extend arm/fist forward (open-closed fist exercise). MM Keep armpit tight from beginning of punch to avoid fist flicking upwards or elbow going out.
  • 1st two knuckles at target; knuckles to forearm straight; arm to torso 90°. ID

Basic punches

  • The main punches are choku-zuki (straight punch), gyaku-zuki (reverse punch), and oi-zuki (lunge punch).
  • Seiken (making punches) for chokuzuki in hachijidachi:
    1. Fists at centerline, together, fingers then thumbs.
    2. Both sides at same time: pullback fist vertical and other fist upwards at halfway point; elbows rub side of body.
    3. Pullback fist twists upwards as going back, other fist twists rests of way forwards till facing down. Fist goes across and then back center of body to hikite position.
    4. Same time as punching, push up head and shoulders down, tighten lats (down full side of body, coming together feeling), straighten back, ...direction come down to hara CoG with breathing (very important). Kanazawa, Te Waza

Other strikes

  • Horizontal (yoko mawashi) or vertical (tate mawashi). MM
  • Conservation of momentum: conduct opponent’s momentum caught from blocking (whether moving back or turning 180 degrees) to propel the counterattack - same hand (bounce off opponent's body), or opposite hand (through body). Kanazawa, Te Waza
  • Harmony in hand/hips/feet; with sabaki, twist outside of attacker's line just before being hit, strike from elbow joint same time as pushing off with leg. Kanazawa, Te Waza

    • Types
    • Uraken (backhand): horizontal, vertical, and hammer
    • Empi uchi (elbow): front, dropping, upwards, backwards, backwards round, front round, and horizontal
    • Open hand: shutō (sword), haitō (ridge), nukite (spear): four-fingered, vertical, horizontal, and inverted
    • Fingers: one-fingered and two-fingered
    • Others: one knuckle punch and bear claw

Blocks
  • Use wrist pronation to guide arm movement RA
  • Elbow remains at same 90 degree angle regardless of block MM
  • Use fleshy part of arm to block before twisting and blocking to prevent hurting bony parts. Kanazawa, Te Waza

Specifics

  • Guiding blocks: twisting/sheer effect (e.g., age-uke and soto-uke). Harai blocks: twist before impact (e.g., uchi-uke and gedan-barai) Kanazawa, Te Waza
  • Inside-out blocks: utilize opposite side of body; ensure ribs pull towards prepping block arm rather than shoulders hunching in. For upwards blocks: use lats for pulling downwards (age-uke, or yamazuki pullback in Bassai Dai) RA
  • Blocks supported with open hand at side of arm ( Empi, Bassai Dai), tip of middle finger is always at wrist bone. For morote-uke-like movements (Heians, Jion), hand is at the elbow. MM
  • For grabbing techniques that extend outwards ( Kanku Sho, Bassai Dai / Sho), never go above shoulder height (always blocking chudan-zuki).

Basic blocks

  • The main blocks are age-uke, soto-uke, uchi-uke, and gedan-barai.

Lower Body

Kicks (keri)

  • Have fist facing up; contracting back muscles facilitates mawashi-geri and yoko-geri. MM

Basic kicks

  • The main kicks are mae-geri, mawashi-geri, and yoko-geri.

Jumps

  • Don't lean or visibly push off for launch; it's about turning hips forward and squeezing legs together; start low, corkscrew
  • Keep knees and calves together
  • Don't look down RA, and land softly

Full body
  • Turn hips sufficiently. When hips pull opposite of facing direction, snapping movement. When hips push in same direction, slamming movement. MM
  • Use diagonal compression when the leg of one side is active, and the arm of the other is active. RA

    • Stepping

    1. Keep core tight to loosen limbs
      • Transitive phase: breathe in, gravity up MM
      • Use the lower body for going forwards and upper body for going backwards; for going backwards, don't start off by reaching with back foot and then moving arm back; drop body and everything moves back at once RA
      • Start each movement with small hip contraction and hand loosening and not from head; control hands and loosen legs RA
      • Hand technique begins with step and with head movement; eliminate delay in arms after legs or head; better to move hands early, to allow more optionality; avoid extra movement with hands or flicking RA
    2. Move directly
      • Striking phase: breathe out, gravity down. Adjust feet in moving through stances to maintain centerline. MM
      • Hit from the one point (four points—hips and shoulders): make sure head / upper body / hands are synchronized with going forward/back; use opposite body; expand back but don't overextend shoulders or overturn; move via core and bending front knee RA
      • Compress in middle of technique for setup
      • Prep for technique should have large or "floating" movement; advance or uncoil toward opponent on axis while always squeezing and without pausing or losing forward momentum; emphasize one point moving forward smoothly rather than pushing off of back leg suddenly RA
      • Hikite bounces off body while going forward RA

    Turning

    • Turn hips and feet at same time, rather than feet first--for any turn >=90 degrees
    • As if presenting chest first (see gedan-barai in Enpi, Rika Usami's axis and rotation for manji-uke in Gankaku)
    • Shift weight slightly during prep to maximize shift back into final stance (see manji-uke in Jion)
