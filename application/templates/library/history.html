Ryūha (Schools)

Okinawan karate (in Okinawan Toudi - for T’ang Dynasty and “hand”), was not based in city-centered (i.e. Shuri, Tomari, Naha) systems. From Toudi came Shotokan, Shito-ryu, Goju-ryu, Shorin-ryu, and Uechi-ryu. The most famous men came from Naha and Shuri, but most karate men in 19th C were Shuri government employees. Naha, Shuri, and Tomari are also less than a 20 minute walk apart. The names Naha-Te, Shuri-Te, and Tomari-Te weren’t given until 1926 (Redmond 23-4).

When Funakoshi learned Karate, his sensei Itosu and Azato only taught kata. Kihon and kumite drills as we know today were nonexistent. It may have been Funakoshi exposure to kendo and its training method that would lead him to introduce gohon kumite - kendo’s uchikomi.

During the early 20th C, the time of Funakoshi, Mabuni, and Miyagi, with the rise of imperialism and nationalism and the decline of interest in China, the word Toudi was gradually replaced by the word Karate, a more Japanese word. T'ang was replaced by the kanji meaning "void", "sky", or "empty." 空手 The kanji for "Kara-Te" means Empty Hand. Funakoshi made karate more Japanese by incorporating elements of judo and kendo (in particular, kendo’s competition style). Most karate practitioners then were kendo experts like Nakayama (Redmond 27, 30).
