Origin

As Robert Redmond writes, karate originated in Okinawa centuries ago but its roots extend to China, Korea, Japan, Taiwan, and other Pacific Rim nations. Most of the exchange was from trade and diplomacy, but at times, visitors unintentionally washed ashore. [1]

China

Oral histories and a few documents describe the strong influence of Chinese martial arts on Okinawa during the 18th and 19th centuries. Those arts historically center around the Shaolin temple in China's Henan province, where martial artists from different styles sought refuge when they were being hunted by the Qing Dynasty (which lasted from 1644-1912). Chinese boxing developed there and spread outwards; these systems, such as White Crane, Five Ancestors, Monk Fist, Dragon Fist, Lion Boxing, Dog Boxing, and Tiger Fist, were what was then transmitted to the Okinawans. [2]

Okinawa

Over the centuries, Okinawans and Chinese freely visited and lived in China's southeastern Fujian province and Okinawa's Naha and Shuri cities. Especially in Fujian's capital Fuzhou, several forms of gongfu (skill through perseverance) or quanfa (literally, fist rule, and kenpo in Japanese) were taught. As Chinese martial artists freely trained under different systems and instructors, those arts evolved over time, while the Okinawans preserved the style they had learned within a given period. [1]

The styles of Okinawan toudi (literally, T’ang Dynasty hand) that emerged from this interaction came directly from the Chinese instructors and their time period, rather than specific Okinawan cities. While most famous toudi practitioners came from Naha (a major port city) and Shuri (the capital), most karate men by the 19th century were Shuri government employees, and the two cities were less than a 20-minute walk apart. Their differences more likely represent different points of time of the same Chinese martial art, rather than different martial arts altogether. The 1926 designation of toudi styles as Naha-Te, Shuri-Te, and Tomari-Te is then an anachronistic attempt to systematize those styles. Chinese instructors

As Redmond writes, the Okinawans' Chinese martial arts instructors, with the exception of Chinto, were "government officials with diplomatic status [who] interacted with their high-ranking and wealthy counterparts from Okinawa." Gichin Funakoshi

Gichin Funakoshi taught toudi for physical education in an elementary school in 1901, and in a high school in 1905. In 1902 Shintaro Ogawa, a school commissioner for Kagoshima prefecture, witnessed an exhibition of toudi and reported to the Japanese Ministry of Education the benefits of toudi. After that, toudi became a part of schools' curricula and began to be practiced freely in Japan (JKA India - chief instructors).

Japan

During the early 20th century, with Japanese imperialism on the rise and its interest in China on the decline, the Japanese term karate gradually replaced the Okinawan term toudi. T'ang Dynasty hand, which was implied in the Okinawan term, became "void", "sky", or "empty". Thus, 空手 (karate) means empty hand. [1]

In this period, technique names were also introduced. Previously, there was no relationship between a technique and a single name, as Okinawan masters did not want the application of a given movement to be constrained by a single name.[1]

Shotokan

Gichin Funakoshi

Gichin Funakoshi, for bringing Shotokan karate to Japan and establishing the first karate dojo, is its greatest influence.[0] He made the Okinawan art more Japanese by incorporating elements from the Japanese arts judo and kendo. [1] From kendo, he absorbed its competition style, and it may have been Funakoshi's exposure to kendo's training method that would lead him to introduce gohon kumite (five-step sparring)-- kendo’s uchikomi. [0] Most of his Japanese students were kendo experts, too, like Masatoshi Nakayama, who would become the second greatest influence on Shotokan karate. [1]
