Kihon

Kihon means basic, but as all experienced karate-ka know, basic is anything but easy. The simplest movements can illustrate the areas requiring the most improvement in one's application of karate, as can techniques isolated from combinations or speed. It is for this reason that the tradition of seemingly endless repetition of techniques exists. As practitioners at one dojo joked, "Mo ichi do means mo bettah" ("one more time means better"). It's true: difference between skill level is often the difference of the sheer amount of time committed. Though at the same time, only "perfect practice that makes perfect technique." Kihon, then, where all karate begins and ends.

This page is separated by the dominant parts of the body used for each technique, though the experienced karate-ka will utilize much more of the body than may be immediately apparent. However, for organization's sake, I've split this page by stances, arm techniques, leg techniques, and the entire body, similarly to how a new student might be ntroduced to stances, blocks, punches, kicks, and then stepping forward with any of those techniques. As this is part of my compendium of notes, I have the more fundamental points embolded, while my personal notes/goals (I'd like to believe that these can be anyone's) are italicized.

Terminology

Some concepts have been described interchangeably (depending on where I was in my life at the time of my notetaking) by anatomical terms (sagittal/lateral—dividing left and right, frontal/coronal—dividing front and back), and transverse—dividing top and bottom), or by Amos Sensei's terms (front body—could mean squeezing the pectorals and lats forward, and opposite body—squeezing the lats and ribs on the opposite side of one's technique forward). It's the same for the terms center of gravity and one point—I believe that one point is more specific than center of gravity since it refers to the position of shoulders and hips in addition to one's tantien, but they otherwise embody the same karate concept of keeping technique low and direct. There may also be conflict between such concepts, as these ideas belong to different instructors and/or organizations, and have thus been superscripted to denote origin/credit.

General Technique

  • Basics

    • Seven important parts of techniques (Scott Langley, previously of WTKO): 1. Breathing; 2. Centerline; 3. Active stability; 4. Maximal contraction/expansion; 5. Snap/thrust; 6. Golgi effect; 7. Hip rotation: front/back
    • Even when doing slower techniques, do not start off slow or delay technique and then speed up— maintain same speed; eventually improve initial acceleration.

  • Coordination

    • The centerline is on medial and frontal planes. By isolating the torso into different working segments, conserve energy and momentum while maintaining the technique’s direction upon the centerline.
    • The body operates as separate units, but all units must end at the same time.
    • Use arms for legs and legs for arms; this doesn't mean unncessary movement, but being mindful of the placement and usage of these parts in tandem with their respective arm/leg techniques.

  • Combinations

    • Pair or triple techniques to bounce from one technique to the next, and emphasize pauses between combinations (See Heian Godan, Bassai)

Stances

  • Placement

    • Knee remains at same angle regardless of stance; only ankle angle changes.
    • Maintain direction of center of gravity's momentum in turns by not bringing the foot back.
    • Squeeze with front and inner thigh when feet are not crossed, otherwise with back and inner thigh.
    • Head up; shoulders down; hara and tailbone pulled towards each other.

  • Transitions

    • High center of gravity = speed; low center of gravity = power. Cats stay low in advancing on prey while prey moves upward (like antelopes) in attempting to escape.
    • Avoid pushing off in opposite direction first or turning out knee/footprior to movement; drop and then move. Emulate Michael Jordan's coiled knees - coil down for transition between stances, moving into coil rather than moving pullback hand back; or coil downwards and towards, contracting ribs (see Heian Godan).

  • Specific Stances

    • Zenkutsu-dachi
    • Kōkutsu-dachi
      • Transfer weight to ball of back foot; like moving back in kumite.
      • Transition between progressive zenkutsu-dachi with koukutsu-dachi, and vice versa.
      • Use hip to drive foot forward and turn; keep knee tight.
      • Hip torques back so that energy moves back instead of forward (Shinji Tanaka, SKIF).
    • Fudō-dachi
      • Keep back knee pointing to side.

Upper Body

  • General

    • Keep shoulders down by using latissimus dorsi muscles.

  • Hikite

    (pullback hand)
    • Use back for hikite instead of arm.
    • Squeeze upper arm and lats together (as if squeezing armpit) for hikite.
    • Hikite is on and protects the soft area between the lowest rib and the hip bone.
    • Hikite is ½-way between armpit & hip, with elbow pulled towards back, back tight, and knuckles to forearm straight. Causes same torso side to contract; other side expands; whips.

  • Timing

    • Better to move hands early, allowing more optionality.

  • Punches

    • 1st two knuckles at target; knuckles to forearm straight; arm to torso 90°.
    • Use lats to fully extend arm/fist forward (open-closed fist exercise).Keep armpit tight from beginning of punch to avoid flicking upwards, and watch punching elbow from going out.
    • Can turn hip in opposite direction first to get hip twist with punch. (,  Germany 2014 demonstration)

  • Specific punches

    • Seiken (making punches) for chokuzuki in hachijidachi (the basic punch; will review video to add clarity to notes):
      1. Fists at centerline, together, fingers then thumbs.
      2. Both sides at same time: pullback fist vertical and other fist upwards at halfway point; elbows rub side of body.
      3. Pullback fist twists upwards as going back, other fist twists rests of way forwards till facing down. Fist goes across and then back center of body to hikite position.
      4. Same time as punching, push up head and shoulders down, tighten lats (down full side of body, coming together feeling, straighten back, ...direction come down to hara CoG with breathing (very important).(, Te Waza)
      Gyakuzuki (Kanazawa, Te Waza)
    • Hips twisting very important, from 45 degrees, hold halfway, don't twist hips and shoulders (front knee can become more obtuse). Then hips, shoulder, and fist twisting at end at same time and breathing out with mouth. Hips/waist don't just twist; they move forward during punch
    • Returning to hikite, breathing inwards through nose (front knee can become more acute as hips go downwards and forwards), pushing back leg towards ground. If fast, breathe out through mouth, same as attacking/punching/blocking.
    • Yahara: gyaku hanmi for gyakuzuki but keep shoulders down, shoulder blades pulled towards back, head facing forward, front knee centered - being fully stretched
      • ○ For jodan uke, ensure shoulder down to keep block’s shape and centered ○ Koukutsudachi: squeeze glute of front leg and squeeze inner thigh of back
    • Turning with punch: emphasis on turning hips and feet at same time, rather than feet first - for any turn >=90 degrees
      • Oi-zuki (Kanazawa, Te Waza):

    Full Body
  • Turn hips sufficiently. When hips pull opposite of facing direction, snapping movement. When hips push in same direction, slamming movement. (Murakami seminar)

    Progressive Movements

    Steps To Moving Forward1

    1. Keep core tight to loosen limbs;
      • Hand technique begins with the step and with head movement, but avoid extra movement with hands or flicking
    2. Make sure head / upper body / hands are synchronized with going forward/back; move and hit from the one point (four points—hips and shoulders) and using the opposite body
      • Use the lower body for going forwards and upper body for going backwards
    3. Forward momentum: Keep everything direct
      • Prep for technique should have large or "floating" movement while always squeezing and without pausing; hikite as if bouncing off body while going forward
      • Emphasize one point moving smoothly forward rather than pushing off of back leg suddenly1
    4. Advance or uncoil toward opponent on axis without pausing or losing forward momentum; expand back but don't overextend shoulders or overturn
    Stepping forward, transitive phase: breathe in, gravity up. Striking phase: breathe out, gravity down. Adjust feet in moving through stances to maintain centerline. (Murakami seminar) compress in middle of technique for setup

    Turning

    • As if presenting chest first (see gedan barai in Enpi, manji-uke in Gankaku, Rika Usami's axis and rotation)
    • Shift weight slightly during prep to maximize shift back into final stance (See Jion manji-uke)
